Walk through the websites of ten cosmetic practices and you'll see the same
pictures: a stock model with impossible skin, a syringe held at a glamorous
angle, a before-and-after grid stretched to the footer. Familiar, easy, and
in Australia, increasingly risky.

Imagery is advertising. The regulator reads your photographs the same way it
reads your headlines, and patients read them more closely than either. Which
makes photography one of the highest-stakes brand decisions a practice makes.

## The problem with perfect skin

Stock photography of flawless faces does two kinds of damage. To the brand,
it says *we couldn't be bothered to show you who we are*. To the patient, it
quietly promises an outcome no clinician would put their name to. Guidance on
advertising for regulated health services warns against creating unreasonable
expectations of beneficial treatment, and idealised imagery is one of the
ways expectations get set.

Honest photography works harder. Real rooms, real hands, real light. A
patient deciding between two practices is reassured by the one that looks
like a place that actually exists.

## Before-and-afters deserve their own rules

Used carefully, before-and-after images are legitimate clinical information.
Used carelessly, they're the fastest way to attract a complaint. The
principles are consistent: like-for-like lighting and angles, no filters or
retouching, genuine patients with genuine consent, and no cherry-picking a
best-case result as though it were typical.

If a gallery can't meet that standard, the stronger move is not to run one.

## What we commission instead

For the practices we work with, the brief is usually the same three sessions:
the clinician at work, unposed; the spaces, shot like architecture; and
texture details — instruments, linen, light — that carry the brand's calm
without making a single claim. None of it dates, none of it needs a
disclaimer, and all of it says the one thing that matters: this practice
takes care.
